By Juliana Ong

Ever thought about the timing of baby-making? Maybe you've observed the surprising amount of , or been suspicious of the similar birth-month patterns shared among you and your siblings. Don’t worry, you’re not alone—the internet is rife with theories regarding the catalysts behind conception.

Some suggest that the cozy warmth of the holidays ignites couples' carnal flame, while others claim that the oppressive heat of summer leads to a decrease in intimacy. There's even speculation that major sports victories boosts national morale, resulting in a baby boom nine months after. But can these urban legends really explain the patterns of conception in an entire country?

The Philippines, long notorious for its burgeoning population, is no stranger to baby-making. While birth rates have seen a gradual decline the past decade, the country still ranks second in Southeast Asia with a birth rate of 22.1, almost triple that of its neighbor Singapore.

To find out the driving force behind these numbers, we analyzed 30 Million baby records from the PSA (Philippine Statistics Authority) from 2006 to 2023 to underpin possible reasons that may significantly impact birth and conception trends in the country.

🌦 Seasonal Patterns

A bird’s eye view of monthly births reveals a clear pattern in the data: September to November see the highest peaks in births, while the driest troughs occur from February to April.

Counting back nine months prior to the season with most births, an amusing observation comes to mind.

Is it true Filipinos love to make-love during Christmas, New Year, and Valentines day?

Interestingly, these ber-month babies might have more to do with climate than the holidays.

Studies show that countries near the equator, like the Philippines, tend to have birth peaks later in the fiscal calendar, and vice versa.

The running hypothesis is that cooler months offer better conditions for reproduction.

While extreme heat and humidity can act as both a behavioral and physiological contraceptive.

People may find sex less desirable when it's hot and stifling outside; bodily factors like sperm and ovarian functions can also be affected by the temperature.

But what about factors beyond seasonal shifts? Could the rumors hold some truth?

To put these theories to the test, we delved into unique events in Philippine history to determine whether they caused a significant change in seasonal conception trends.

Each analysis below compares different years from a particular month to identify whether certain events resulted in significant birth trend outliers. Monthly percent changes were used instead of raw birth numbers to make data independent of the global decline in birth rates, resulting in a fairer analysis.

🥊 Big Sports Victories

If anything can stir national fervor among Filipinos, it's an epic sports win. And who better to embody this than Philippines' boxing legend (and almost-president) Manny Pacquiao?

On December 8, 2008, millions of Filipinos worldwide beamed with pride as Pacquiao clinched an underdog victory against the highly decorated 11-time world titleholder, Oscar De La Hoya. Dubbed "The Dream Match," this fight propelled Pacquiao to full-blown superstar status as De La Hoya symbolically "passed the torch" to the Filipino boxer, marking a historic night in Philippine sporting victories.

A glance at the data nine months after this victory shows a 15% change in births in September of 2009, the highest compared to Septembers from other years.

Perhaps this Pacquiao match birthed a couple of boxing-babies. But what was the extent?

September 2009's percent-change falls within the highlighted area between the lower and upper fences, indicating it's within the expected range of variation.

While there might be a slight increase, it's not extreme enough to be considered an outlier or to suggest a significant baby boom.

💰 Corruption Scandals

Another event that sparked public outrage was the infamous Pork Barrel Scam—a colossal fraud by high-profile members of Congress, involving the theft of over 10 billion pesos and the misuse of the Priority Development Assistance Fund (PDAF), a discretionary fund intended for development projects.

To grasp the gravity of this scam, consider that it would take the average Filipino 18,621 years of labor to earn back that amount.

The intense focus on the Pork Barrel scam created socio-political stress and uncertainty, potentially influencing the 23.35% change in births on February 2015, nine months after the scandal’s peak on May of the previous year.

Based on birth trends from February, we know it to be a month of negative percent changes. Despite February 2015 recording the steepest number, it still falls within the normal range of figures and is consistent with the typical patterns we've observed over the past 17 years.

Perhaps the scandal's effects on conception wasn't as big as we initially thought.

🌪 Natural Calamities

Instead of events centered around human personalities, maybe the fury of nature will cause a baby boom!

Rumor has it that prolonged isolation under dim lights often results in couples finding other ways to pass time … leading to a shared responsibility 9 months after.

Contrary to popular belief, major storms like Typhoons Ondoy and Yolanda didn't lead to a baby boom in the Philippines. Birth data 9 months after showed no clear trend: Ondoy may have caused a slight increase, while Yolanda had no noticeable impact. In both cases, figures stayed within the normal range.

Given the Philippines' archipelagic nature, a more detailed regional analysis might reveal localized effects. Unfortunately, such data wasn't available for our study.

⛑ Global Health Crisis

If none of the above had an effect magnitudes enough to influence a unique trend in Philippine conception, then surely a global health pandemic should do the trick, right?

Early in the Covid-19 pandemic, more than 108 million Filipinos were confined within in their homes as uncertainty and fear loomed across the world.

Death tolls shot up, businesses were forced to mass shutdowns, and global economies experienced a fatal beating–many couples decided to put a halt to reproduction.

Births recorded nine months after the pandemic’s onset reflected this fear, with data falling down to 16.9% on December 2020, the first significant outlier spotted in this analysis.

However, as COVID-19 continued its global havoc, the initial dip in births proved short-term.

Surprisingly, we observed a positive 16.5% change in March 2021. This suggests that conception rates rebounded sharply in June 2020, just three months into quarantine.

Increased time indoors coupled with the unavailability of contraceptives and reproductive health services among the maginalized sector, eventually contributed to the uptick of both planned and unplanned births.

What do these findings reveal about the factors influencing conception in the Philippines? For the most part, they suggest that baby-making decisions remain largely unpredictable.

While theories such as sports victories and corruption scandals may seem like it influenced a number of conceptions in the country, it’s essential to remember that correlation does not imply causation. Just because two things happen around the same time doesn’t mean one caused the other. Ultimately, the timing of conception in the country seems to be dependent on the individual whims of Filipino couples, but understanding these nuances could bring us closer to identifying genuine causes.

One thing is certain though, people will continue to have children, come rain or shine, victory or defeat, scandal or celebration–and the quest to understand underlying reasons will continue to inspire data-driven explorations like this one.
